1b) Batch audit — score every screen at once

Auditing a whole app? Drop several screenshots and ClearSight scores each one across all six analysis axes — WCAG 2 text contrast, hidden color-vision failures, APCA perceptual contrast, and WCAG 1.4.1 color-only collisions — then ranks the riskiest screens first. No screenshots handy? Record an app walkthrough instead: share a window and click through your product — ClearSight keeps one frame per distinct screen you visit (up to 12, skipping repeats and mid-navigation frames), scores them all, and simultaneously scans the motion between screens for WCAG 2.3.1 flashing. One recording produces both the portfolio debt map and a seizure-safety verdict. Everything runs locally; nothing is uploaded.

Shipping continuously? The identical six-axis audit also runs headlessly in CI: npm run audit -- screens/*.png --min-grade B scores PNG screenshots in pure Node; add --baseline main-audit.json to fail only when a route regresses, and --sarif to publish findings as GitHub code-scanning annotations, or --markdown / --badge for a PR comment and score badge — no browser, zero dependencies.

    1c · Specialist lab Animation & video flash safety Scan GIFs, videos, and motion for WCAG 2.3.1 seizure risk.

    Moving UI can be dangerous in a way no static audit sees: content that flashes more than three times per second across a large area can trigger photosensitive seizures (WCAG 2.3.1 Three Flashes or Below Threshold). Load an animated GIF, APNG, or WebP — or a real MP4/WebM/MOV video such as a screen recording, product demo, or hero banner (sampled at 10 fps, first 24 s) — and ClearSight measures both general-luminance and saturated-red flash rates per screen region plus the area flashing simultaneously, entirely on-device. The 8×8 scan follows WCAG's independent luminance and CIE 1976 UCS chromaticity thresholds.

    No animation or video scanned yet. Danger zone: more than 3 flashes per second covering 25% or more of the frame.

    1d · Specialist lab Keyboard focus evidence Compare focus states or map a tab journey for WCAG 2.4.7 / 2.4.13.

    A focus indicator only exists while focus is present, so no single screenshot can prove WCAG 2.4.7 Focus Visible — it normally sits in the "requires manual testing" list. Two screenshots change that: capture the same view without and with keyboard focus and ClearSight diffs them pixel-by-pixel — the change is the indicator. It is then measured against the WCAG 2.4.13 Focus Appearance bar: a contrasting area at least as large as a 1 px perimeter of the component, at a ≥3:1 contrast between the focused and unfocused states. Both frames stay on-device, and a measured pair moves 2.4.7 out of the conformance summary's manual-testing list.

    No focus pair analyzed yet. Load the same view unfocused and focused (or run a demo) to measure the focus indicator.

        UI component contrast (WCAG 1.4.11)

        Text contrast is not the whole story: a button or input whose surface nearly matches the page has no visible boundary, even when its label passes AA. ClearSight marches outward from each detected region to find the adjacent color and flags component surfaces below the 3:1 non-text contrast minimum.           Tap target size (WCAG 2.5.8)

          No color lens can see this failure: a row of crisp 16px icon buttons passes every contrast check while staying nearly impossible to tap. ClearSight measures each resolved control surface and compact icon block to pixel precision, converts sizes to CSS pixels, and flags targets under the 24×24px minimum whose 24px clearance circle collides with a neighboring target — while honestly honoring the WCAG spacing exception when an undersized control has room around it.

              Color-only distinction risk (WCAG 1.4.1)

              Contrast math checks text against its background — it never asks whether two colors that encode different meanings (status dots, chart lines, buttons) stay tellable-apart. ClearSight projects every distinct dominant-color pair through all seven color-vision matrices and flags pairs that collapse into near-identical shades.

              Render simulations to scan dominant color pairs for color-vision collisions.

                Accessible recolor preview

                ClearSight can repaint your screenshot with the closest replacement colors that fix failing pairings — see the accessible version of your own UI before touching code.

                Every checked pair is evaluated through three independent lenses: WCAG 2 ratio math (AA/AAA/large-text), a seven-mode color-vision projection, and the APCA perceptual contrast score from the WCAG 3 draft. A pair that passes today's ratio math but reads poorly in practice — like light gray on black — is flagged as a perceptual false pass instead of slipping through.

                Tip: type `#` hex, `rgb()/rgba()`, `hsl()/hsla()`, or CSS color names. Every check also projects the pair through all seven color-vision deficiency modes, so you catch pairs that pass WCAG for typical vision but fail for color-blind users.
